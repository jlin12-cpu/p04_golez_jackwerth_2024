\begin{abstract}
We replicate Figure 1, Figure 2, and Table 1 from \cite{golez2024holding}.
Figure 1 plots 12-month maturity interest rates from three sources:
option-implied rates derived from S\&P 500 index options,
zero curve rates from OptionMetrics, and constant maturity Treasury rates from FRED.
Figure 2 plots cumulative wealth indices for a \$1 investment in the dividend strip
and the market from January 1996 through December 2022.
Table 1 reports annualized summary statistics for monthly dividend strip returns
and market returns over the period 1996--2022.
Following the paper's core methodology, we estimate option-implied interest rates
using the outer product approach for 1996--2003 and the regression approach for 2004--2022,
and we construct strip prices from put-call parity using these implied rates.
Our replication of Figure 1 closely matches the paper's findings,
with an average implied-minus-zero spread of 8.15 basis points
versus 7 basis points reported in the paper.
For Figure 2, our replicated market terminal values are reasonably close to the paper
across all three panels, while strip terminal values are materially higher,
consistent with the higher mean strip return documented in Table 1.
For Table 1, our replicated market return statistics are close to the paper,
while strip return statistics show somewhat larger deviations,
likely due to differences between our automated CRSP-based dividend construction
and the paper's Datastream-based implementation.
We extend all results through 2024 and find that the option-implied rate
becomes lower than the zero curve rate on average in the post-2022 period.
For the extended version of Table 1, market return moments remain broadly stable,
whereas strip return moments become much more extreme in 2023--2024.
We interpret the latter pattern as likely reflecting increased sensitivity
of strip-price construction to end-of-day option quotes during a period
of unusually large interest rate movements.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\cite{golez2024holding} estimate short-term dividend strip prices
from 27 years of S\&P 500 index options data covering 1996--2022.
A key methodological contribution of the paper is the use of
option-implied interest rates rather than exogenous zero curve rates
when estimating strip prices.
The authors show that even a small error of 6 basis points in the interest rate
can bias half-annual dividend strip returns by 0.86\%.

Figure 1 of the paper plots three 12-month maturity interest rate series:
the option-implied rate, the zero curve rate from OptionMetrics,
and the constant maturity Treasury rate from FRED.
The paper reports that the option-implied rate is on average 7 basis points
higher than the zero curve rate over the full sample.

Figure 2 of the paper plots cumulative wealth indices for a \$1 investment
in the dividend strip and the market from January 1996 through December 2022,
across three panels corresponding to raw returns, returns in excess of the
risk-free rate, and returns in excess of duration-matched Treasury bond returns.

Table 1 of the paper reports annualized summary statistics for monthly
dividend strip returns and market returns, including raw returns,
returns in excess of the risk-free rate, and returns in excess of
duration-matched Treasury bond returns.
The paper's main finding is that short-term dividend strips deliver
Sharpe ratios comparable to or higher than those of the market,
despite much higher short-horizon return volatility.
The strong negative autocorrelation of strip returns is interpreted
as evidence of measurement error in strip prices estimated from options data.

In this replication, we reproduce Figure 1, Figure 2, and Table 1
using WRDS-accessed OptionMetrics and CRSP data together with
Treasury rate data from FRED and Kenneth French's data library.
Because this course project emphasizes an end-to-end reproducible analytical pipeline,
our implementation follows the paper's core methodology closely
while adapting certain data inputs to fit a fully automated WRDS-based workflow.
We then extend all results through 2024.

\section{Data}

We use data from the following sources.

\textbf{SPX options.}
We use European S\&P 500 index options from OptionMetrics via WRDS,
covering January 1996 through December 2024.
Following the paper, we use options observed on the last business day of each month,
with maturity of at least 90 days and bid and ask quotes both at or above \$3.
We further restrict the sample to options with moneyness between 0.5 and 1.5.
One important difference from the paper is that the original study uses intraday CBOE data
between 10 a.m.\ and 2 p.m., whereas our replication uses end-of-day OptionMetrics data.
This difference likely accounts for part of the remaining gap between our estimates
and the published values.

\textbf{Zero curve rates.}
We obtain daily zero curve rates from OptionMetrics.
These rates are expressed in continuously compounded form and are linearly interpolated
to obtain a 1-year constant-maturity zero curve rate.

\textbf{Treasury rates.}
We obtain the 1-year constant maturity Treasury rate from FRED
(series DGS1) and convert it to continuously compounded form.
For excess return calculations in Table 1,
we obtain monthly returns on 2-year and 10-year fixed maturity Treasuries from CRSP.

\textbf{Market return proxy and dividend proxy.}
We obtain daily S\&P 500 index data from CRSP.
To construct our automated monthly market panel,
we aggregate daily returns to the monthly frequency and use a cleaned
CRSP-based monthly market return series as our market return proxy.
We also construct a monthly dividend proxy from CRSP-based return data.
This dividend series is used in the strip return calculation and serves
as an automated proxy for the realized monthly dividend required by the paper's methodology.

\textbf{Risk-free rate.}
We obtain the monthly 1-month T-bill rate from Kenneth French's data library.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Option-Implied Interest Rates}

Following Section 2.1 of the paper, we estimate option-implied interest rates
using two methods.

\textbf{Outer Product Approach (1996--2003).}
For each pair of strike prices $(X_i, X_j)$ where $X_i > X_j$, we compute
\begin{equation}
r_f
=
-\frac{1}{\tau}
\ln\left[
\frac{(P(X_i)-C(X_i))-(P(X_j)-C(X_j))}{X_i-X_j}
\right].
\end{equation}
We then take the median across all valid strike pairs for a given date and maturity.

\textbf{Regression Approach (2004--2022).}
For later years, we estimate
\begin{equation}
S_t - C(X) + P(X) = P^{\tau} + \beta X + \epsilon,
\end{equation}
where $\beta = e^{-r_f \tau}$.
The implied interest rate is then recovered as
\begin{equation}
r_f = -\frac{1}{\tau}\ln \hat{\beta}.
\end{equation}

For both the option-implied rate and the zero curve rate,
we linearly interpolate across maturities to obtain a 1-year constant-maturity series.

\subsection{Dividend Strip Prices and Returns}

Following Section 2 of the paper, we estimate the price of a dividend strip
from put-call parity:
\begin{equation}
P_t^{\tau} = S_t + p_t^{\tau}(X) - c_t^{\tau}(X) - X e^{-r_f \tau},
\end{equation}
where $r_f$ is the option-implied interest rate estimated above.
For each put-call pair at the same strike, we obtain one estimate of $P_t^{\tau}$,
and we take the median across all pairs as the strip price for that date and maturity.

We follow the paper's rolling strategy, fixing the strip maturity at approximately
2 years using options expiring in June or December.
Specifically, for months January through June we use the December expiration
approximately 1.9 years away, and for months July through December we use
the June expiration approximately 1.9 years away.
Each contract is held for approximately 6 months before rolling over.

Monthly strip log returns are computed as
\begin{equation}
r_t^{\text{Strip}} = \ln\left(\frac{P_t + D_t}{P_{t-1}}\right),
\end{equation}
where $P_t$ is the price of the held contract at month end and $D_t$ is a monthly
dividend proxy constructed from CRSP data.
Monthly market log returns are computed from our cleaned CRSP-based monthly market series,
which serves as an automated proxy for the market return used in the paper.

\subsection{Table 1 Replication}

Table 1 replicates Table 1 from \cite{golez2024holding},
reporting annualized summary statistics for monthly dividend strip returns
and market returns over the period 1996--2022.

Our replicated market return statistics are close to the paper.
The annualized mean market return is 8.20\% in our replication
versus 8.54\% in the paper, and the annualized standard deviation
is 16.25\% versus 15.68\%.
The market Sharpe ratio in excess of the risk-free rate is 0.38 in our replication
versus 0.42 in the paper.

For strip returns, the replicated Sharpe ratio of 0.18 is close to the paper's 0.16,
and the negative AR(1) of $-0.37$ is close to the paper's value of $-0.33$.
These results support the paper's main qualitative finding that short-term
dividend strips deliver Sharpe ratios comparable to the market despite substantially
higher short-horizon return volatility.
The somewhat higher mean strip return and standard deviation in our replication
(9.34\% and 40.46\% versus 7.10\% and 31.98\%) likely reflect differences between
our automated CRSP-based dividend construction and the paper's Datastream-based implementation.

\subsubsection{Figure 2 Extension}

Figure \ref{fig:figure2_extended_winsorized} extends Figure 2 through 2024.
Extending the sample using the fully automated pipeline produces implausibly
large cumulative strip returns, driven by several extreme post-2022 monthly
strip returns.
Diagnostic checks suggest that this instability is driven by large jumps
in the estimated strip-price series, likely reflecting put-call parity violations
or other quote-quality issues in OptionMetrics end-of-day data for long-dated options
after 2022, rather than by the plotting logic itself.
As a robustness check, we winsorize post-2022 strip log returns at $\pm 0.50$.
This materially reduces the explosive late-sample behavior, although the strip
still substantially outperforms the market in the extended sample, suggesting
that late-sample strip estimates remain unstable and should be interpreted
with caution.
For readability, we report the winsorized robustness version in the main text,
since the raw extended strip series is dominated by a small number of extreme late-sample observations.

\subsubsection{Table 1 Extension}

The extended version of Table 1 shows that market return statistics remain broadly stable
relative to the paper sample, with the annualized mean increasing slightly to 9.08\%
and the Sharpe ratio in excess of the risk-free rate rising to 0.43.

Strip return statistics change substantially in the extended sample.
The annualized mean strip return increases to 18.29\%
and the standard deviation rises to 66.50\%.
The AR(1) of strip returns weakens from $-0.37$ to $-0.14$.
These changes are driven by several extreme strip return observations in 2023--2024.
We interpret these results as likely reflecting increased sensitivity of the strip
construction to end-of-day option quotes during a period of unusually large
interest rate movements, rather than a stable shift in the economics of dividend strips.
We therefore view the extended strip results as exploratory rather than fully stable.

\section{Conclusion}

We replicate Figure 1, Figure 2, and Table 1 from \cite{golez2024holding}
using OptionMetrics end-of-day data together with CRSP and FRED data.
Our Figure 1 results are numerically close to the published values,
with remaining differences likely attributable to differences between
our end-of-day data construction and the paper's intraday CBOE-based implementation.

Figure 2 cumulative return patterns are qualitatively consistent with the paper,
with market terminal values closely matching the published figure across all three panels.
Strip terminal values are higher in our replication, consistent with the
higher mean strip return in Table 1 and attributable to the same data source difference.

For Table 1, our market return statistics are close to the paper,
while strip return statistics show somewhat larger deviations.
Even so, our replication captures the paper's main qualitative pattern:
strip returns exhibit much higher volatility and strongly negative autocorrelation,
while maintaining a Sharpe ratio close to that of the market.
In particular, our replicated strip Sharpe ratio in excess of the risk-free rate
is 0.18, compared with 0.16 in the paper.

The extension through 2024 shows that the option-implied rate becomes lower than
the zero curve rate on average in the post-2022 period,
while the strip-return extension becomes much less stable.
Market return moments remain broadly similar in the extended sample,
but strip return moments are heavily influenced by several extreme observations
in 2023--2024.
A winsorized robustness check confirms that the explosive late-sample strip behavior
is driven largely by a small number of extreme monthly observations, although the
extended strip series remains considerably less stable than the market series.
Overall, the extension suggests that the paper's interest-rate finding may not be stable
in recent years, while the extension of strip returns is more sensitive
to the practical details of options-based strip construction.
